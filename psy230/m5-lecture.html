Independent-Samples t-Test Logic

Use this test when you have two separate groups of participants. No one is in both groups. There is no matching.

Between-Subjects Design

This is a "Between-Subjects" design because we are comparing differences between two different groups of subjects.

  • Example: Group A uses Apple computers. Group B uses PCs. We compare their average typing speed.
  • Contrast with Paired: In paired, the *same* person uses an Apple, then uses a PC. In independent, they only use one.

The Hypotheses

We are comparing the means of two unknown populations ($\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$).

Two-Tailed (Non-Directional)

Question: Is there a difference between the groups?

$H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2$ (or $\mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0$)

$H_1: \mu_1 \neq \mu_2$

One-Tailed (Directional)

Question: Is Group 1 higher than Group 2?

$H_0: \mu_1 \le \mu_2$

$H_1: \mu_1 > \mu_2$

The Sampling Distribution

In Module 4 (Paired), we looked at a distribution of mean differences.
In Module 5 (Independent), we look at a distribution of differences between means.

It sounds the same, but the math is harder because we have two different sources of error (Group 1's variance + Group 2's variance).

Key Concept: The Comparison Distribution is Centered at Zero
Under the null hypothesis, the mean of the comparison distribution (distribution of differences between means) is always 0. Why? Because $H_0$ states that $\mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0$. If the populations are truly equal, we'd expect the difference between sample means to average out to zero across many samples.

The Concept of "Pooled Variance"

This is the most critical calculation concept in Module 5.

Why do we "Pool"?

We have two samples. Sample 1 has a variance ($s_1^2$). Sample 2 has a variance ($s_2^2$).

We assume the populations they come from actually have the same variance (Assumption of Homogeneity of Variance). To get the best estimate of this shared variance, we combine (pool) our estimates.

Why not just average them?
If Group 1 has 100 people and Group 2 has 5 people, Group 1's variance is a much more reliable estimate. We calculate a Weighted Average based on Degrees of Freedom ($df$).

Formulas You Need

1. Degrees of Freedom per Group:

$df_1 = n_1 - 1$

$df_2 = n_2 - 1$

$df_{total} = df_1 + df_2$ (or $N_{total} - 2$)

Reading t(df) Notation: Finding Sample Size

In research articles and quiz questions, you'll see results reported as $t(df) = value$.

Example: $t(19) = 4.02, p = .01$

  • The number in parentheses (19) is the degrees of freedom
  • For independent-samples: $df_{total} = N_{total} - 2$
  • Therefore: $N_{total} = df + 2 = 19 + 2 = \mathbf{21}$ participants total

Quiz Tip: If a question asks "What is the sample size given $t(19)$?" the answer is 21 (add 2 to the df for independent-samples t-tests).

Knowledge Check: Sample Size from t-Notation

The results of an independent-samples t-test were reported as: $t(24) = 2.15, p = .04$

What was the total sample size (N)?

N = 26

For independent-samples t-tests: $N = df + 2 = 24 + 2 = 26$

Remember: Independent-samples uses $df = N - 2$ (we lose 1 df per group), so reverse it by adding 2.

2. Pooled Variance ($s_{pooled}^2$):

$s_{pooled}^2 = \frac{df_1}{df_{total}}(s_1^2) + \frac{df_2}{df_{total}}(s_2^2)$

(Note: You might also see this written as sum of squares divided by total df. Both work. This weighted average formula is often easier to type into a calculator).

Reality Check: Your $s_{pooled}^2$ must fall between $s_1^2$ and $s_2^2$. If your answer is higher than both or lower than both, you made a math error!

The Independent Calculation Engine

This is the longest manual calculation in the course. Follow these 6 steps carefully.

Practice Scenario: Caffeine Reaction Time

Research: Does caffeine improve reaction time? (One-tailed test, $\alpha=.05$)

Group 1 (Caffeine): $n=5$, Mean ($M_1$) = 4, Variance ($s_1^2$) = 2.5

Group 2 (Decaf): $n=5$, Mean ($M_2$) = 7, Variance ($s_2^2$) = 1.5

Prediction: Caffeine Group (1) will have lower time than Decaf Group (2). $H_1: \mu_1 < \mu_2$.

Step 1: Calculate df

$df_1 = 5 - 1 = 4$

$df_2 = 5 - 1 = 4$

$df_{total} = 4 + 4 = \mathbf{8}$

Step 2: Calculate Pooled Variance ($s_{pooled}^2$)

We weigh the variances by their df.

$s_{pooled}^2 = \frac{4}{8}(2.5) + \frac{4}{8}(1.5)$

$s_{pooled}^2 = 0.5(2.5) + 0.5(1.5)$

$s_{pooled}^2 = 1.25 + 0.75 = \mathbf{2.0}$

Step 3: Variance of Sampling Distributions ($s_{M}^2$)

We assume the pooled variance applies to both, but we divide by their specific sample sizes.

$s_{M1}^2 = \frac{s_{pooled}^2}{n_1} = \frac{2.0}{5} = 0.4$

$s_{M2}^2 = \frac{s_{pooled}^2}{n_2} = \frac{2.0}{5} = 0.4$

Step 4: Variance of Differences ($s_{diff}^2$)

Add the two sampling variances together.

$s_{diff}^2 = 0.4 + 0.4 = \mathbf{0.8}$

Step 5: Standard Deviation of Difference ($s_{diff}$)

Also known as the Standard Error of Difference.

$s_{diff} = \sqrt{0.8} = \mathbf{0.894}$

(This is your denominator for t)

Step 6: Calculate t

$t = \frac{M_1 - M_2}{s_{diff}}$

$t = \frac{4 - 7}{0.894} = \frac{-3}{0.894} = \mathbf{-3.36}$

Assignment Note: In your assignment, the sample sizes ($n$) are different. This means your weights in Step 2 will not be 50/50. Pay close attention to the fractions!

Decision & Effect Size

1. The Decision

Using the Caffeine example above ($t = -3.36$, $df_{total} = 8$, One-tailed).

  1. Go to t-table. Look up $df=8$.
  2. One-tailed, $\alpha=.05$.
  3. Critical value = 1.860.
  4. Since we predicted Group 1 < Group 2, our critical t is -1.860.
  5. Comparison: Is -3.36 more extreme than -1.860? Yes.
  6. Result: Reject Null. Caffeine significantly reduces reaction time.

2. Effect Size (Cohen's d)

For independent samples, we compare the difference in means to the Pooled Standard Deviation.

$d = \frac{M_1 - M_2}{\sqrt{s_{pooled}^2}}$

Note: The denominator is the square root of pooled variance. Do NOT use the standard error ($s_{diff}$) here.

Calculate d for Caffeine Study

Mean Difference = $4 - 7 = -3$

Pooled Variance ($s_{pooled}^2$) = 2.0

Pooled Std Dev ($s_{pooled}$) = $\sqrt{2.0} = 1.414$

$d = \frac{-3}{1.414} = \mathbf{-2.12}$

Interpretation: Huge effect (d > 0.8).

What Does Cohen's d Actually Mean?
Cohen's d tells you how many pooled standard deviations apart the two group means are.
  • $d = 0.50$ means the means are 0.50 standard deviations apart
  • $d = 1.24$ means the means are 1.24 standard deviations apart

This is why effect size is so useful â€” it puts the difference on a standardized scale that can be compared across studies.

Cohen's d Interpretation Guidelines (Memorize These!)

Effect Size (|d|) Interpretation
0.2 Small effect
0.5 Medium effect
0.8 Large effect

Note: Use the absolute value of d for interpretation. A d of -0.60 is a "medium" effect (the negative just indicates direction).

Knowledge Check: Effect Size Denominator

When calculating Cohen's d for an independent-samples t-test, what goes in the denominator?

The Pooled Standard Deviation ($s_{pooled}$).

Many students make the mistake of using the Standard Error ($s_{diff}$) used in the t-test. Effect size is about the populations (standard deviation), not the sampling precision (standard error).

3. p-Values and Confidence Intervals

The p-Value and Confidence Interval Connection
There's a direct relationship between the significance level ($\alpha$) and confidence intervals:
Alpha Level (p) Confidence Interval
$\alpha = .05$ 95% CI
$\alpha = .01$ 99% CI
$\alpha = .10$ 90% CI

The pattern: Confidence Level = $1 - \alpha$ (expressed as a percentage).

Quiz Tip: If asked "A p-level of 0.05 corresponds to a confidence interval of ___%", the answer is 95%.

Synthesizing the t-Tests

You have now learned all three t-tests. One of the main goals of this module is distinguishing between them.

Test Type Research Design Comparison Population Variance Used
One-Sample t One group compared to a known standard (e.g., National Avg). $\mu$ is known. Sample variance ($s^2$).
Paired-Samples t One group tested twice (Pre/Post) OR Matched Pairs. $\mu_{diff} = 0$ (Null is zero difference). Variance of difference scores ($s_{diff}^2$).
Independent-Samples t Two separate, unrelated groups (e.g., Men vs Women). $\mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0$ (Null is zero difference). Pooled Variance ($s_{pooled}^2$).

Assumptions for All t-Tests

  1. Scale Data: The DV must be interval or ratio.
  2. Normality: The populations should be normally distributed (robust if N > 30).
  3. Random Sampling: Ideal, though often violated.
  4. Homogeneity of Variance (New for Independent): We assume Population 1 and Population 2 have similar variances. (That's why we pool them!).

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Using Standard Error instead of Standard Deviation for Cohen's d
    • $s_{diff}$ (standard error) goes in the t-statistic denominator
    • $s_{pooled}$ (pooled SD) goes in the Cohen's d denominator
  2. Forgetting to take the square root
    • Pooled Variance ($s_{pooled}^2$) must become Pooled SD ($s_{pooled}$) for Cohen's d
    • $s_{diff}^2$ must become $s_{diff}$ for the t-statistic
  3. Wrong critical value column
    • One-tailed test â†’ Use one-tailed column
    • Two-tailed test â†’ Use two-tailed column
  4. Unequal sample sizes
    • When $n_1 \neq n_2$, you CANNOT just average the variances
    • You MUST use the weighted formula: $\frac{df_1}{df_{total}}(s_1^2) + \frac{df_2}{df_{total}}(s_2^2)$
Final formatting tip: Round to the hundredth (2 decimal places) as requested in the assignment instructions. Consistent rounding makes your work easier to follow and grade!
