When You Don't See a Bell Curve

The tests we learned previously (t-tests, ANOVA) are Parametric Tests. They rely on the parameters of a normal distribution (Mean and Variance).

The Problem with Categories

Imagine a survey: "Who would you vote for?"

SubjectCandidate
1A
2A
3B

Question: What is the "Mean" candidate? Is it A.5?

Answer: You cannot calculate a mean for Nominal/Categorical data. Therefore, you cannot use t-tests.

Parametric vs. Non-Parametric

Feature Parametric (t-tests, ANOVA) Non-Parametric (Chi-Square)
Variable Type Scale / Continuous / Quantitative Nominal / Categorical / Qualitative
Key Stat Mean ($\mu$, $M$) Frequency / Counts ($f$)
Assumption Normal Distribution (or $N > 30$) Distribution Free (Any shape)
Checklist for using Chi-Square ($X^2$):
  • Are you counting people/items in categories? (Yes)
  • Does calculating an average make no sense? (Yes)
  • Is the data "bumpy" or "lopsided" (not a bell curve)? (Yes)

Chi-Square Goodness of Fit (One Variable)

This test analyzes one variable to see if the breakdown of frequencies fits a specific pattern (usually "equal popularity").

The Logic: Observed vs. Expected

  • Observed Frequency ($O$): The actual count from your survey (Real Life).
  • Expected Frequency ($E$): The count you should get if the Null Hypothesis were true (Theoretical).

Example: Ice Cream Popularity

Research Question: Are Chocolate, Vanilla, and Strawberry equally popular?

Total Customers ($N$): 210

Categories ($k$): 3

How to find Expected Frequency ($E$):
If they are equally popular, we divide the total evenly.
$E = N / k = 210 / 3 = 70$.

(So, we expect 70 people to choose each flavor).

Hypotheses

  • Null ($H_0$): The data fit the expected distribution (No difference in popularity).
  • Alternative ($H_1$): The data do NOT fit the expected distribution (Preferences differ).
$\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}$

Quick Check: Expected Frequency

You survey 100 people about their favorite season (Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter). If the null hypothesis assumes no preference, what is the Expected Frequency ($E$) for "Winter"?

E = 25

$N = 100$, Categories = 4.

$E = 100 / 4 = 25$.

Chi-Square Test for Independence (Two Variables)

This test analyzes two variables to see if they are related (dependent) or unrelated (independent).

Example: Does Candidate Preference depend on Age Group?

The Contingency Table

We arrange data in a grid (Rows $\times$ Columns).

Candidate ACandidate BRow Total
Young503080
Old203050
Col Total7060130 (N)

Hypotheses

  • Null ($H_0$): Variables are Independent. (Age does NOT affect voting).
  • Alternative ($H_1$): Variables are Not Independent. (Voting depends on Age).

Calculating Expected Frequency ($E$) for Independence

This is the hardest part. You calculate a specific $E$ for every single cell based on the row and column totals.

$E = \frac{(\text{Row Total}) \times (\text{Column Total})}{\text{Grand Total}}$

Example Calculation

For the "Young / Candidate A" cell (Top-Left):

  • Row Total (Young) = 80
  • Column Total (Cand A) = 70
  • Grand Total ($N$) = 130

$E = (80 \times 70) / 130 = 5600 / 130 = \mathbf{43.08}$

Logic Check: The Expected Frequency represents the count we would get if the two groups were perfectly proportional (no relationship).

The Calculation Engine

The formula for Chi-Square is the same for both tests. It measures the "distance" between what we saw ($O$) and what we expected ($E$).

$\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}$

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

Let's calculate specific values from the Ice Cream example.

Observed ($O$): Chocolate=15, Vanilla=5
Expected ($E$): 10 each (if Total=20, 2 flavors)

1. Subtract (O - E)

Choc: $15 - 10 = 5$

Van: $5 - 10 = -5$

Note: These usually sum to zero.

2. Square the Difference $(O - E)^2$

Choc: $5^2 = 25$

Van: $(-5)^2 = 25$

(This removes negatives).

3. Divide by Expected $\frac{(O - E)^2}{E}$

Choc: $25 / 10 = 2.5$

Van: $25 / 10 = 2.5$

(This normalizes the error relative to the size of the group).

4. Sum ($\sum$)

$\chi^2 = 2.5 + 2.5 = \mathbf{5.0}$

Common Mistake: Do NOT sum the $(O-E)^2$ column first. You must divide each row by its specific $E$ before summing the results.

Practice: Calculate One Cell

Observed = 20, Expected = 15. What is the contribution to Chi-Square?

1. $O - E = 20 - 15 = 5$

2. Square it: $25$

3. Divide by E: $25 / 15 = \mathbf{1.67}$

Degrees of Freedom & Decisions

Just like t-tests, we compare our Calculated $\chi^2$ to a Critical Value from a table.

Calculating Degrees of Freedom ($df$)

Test Type Formula Example
Goodness of Fit $df = k - 1$
(where $k$ = number of categories)		 4 flavors of ice cream:
$df = 4 - 1 = 3$
Independence $df = (Rows - 1)(Cols - 1)$ 2 Genders $\times$ 3 Majors:
$(2-1)(3-1) = 1 \times 2 = 2$
Note: $df$ depends on the number of categories, NOT the sample size ($N$). A study with $N=50$ and $N=5000$ will have the same $df$ if they both just check "Yes/No."

The Decision Rule

This is consistent with all previous modules:

  • If Calculated $\chi^2$ > Critical Value â†’ Reject Null (the statistic is "more extreme"). Result is significant.
  • If Calculated $\chi^2$ < Critical Value â†’ Fail to Reject (the statistic is "less extreme"). Result is not significant.

The final step is translating the statistical decision into a sentence that answers the research question. Use these templates:

Reject "The categories are not equally popular" or "The distribution differs significantly from expected." "[Variable A] significantly depends on [Variable B]" or "The distribution of [A] differs significantly between [B groups]."
