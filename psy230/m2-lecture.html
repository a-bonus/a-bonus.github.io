From Description to Inference

In Module 1, we learned descriptive statistics — how to summarize data we have. Now we move to inferential statistics — using sample data to draw conclusions about larger populations we can't fully measure.

The Core Problem of Research: We want to know something about a large group (population), but we can only study a small group (sample). Inferential statistics uses probability to bridge this gap — telling us how confident we can be in our conclusions.

Sample vs. Population

This distinction is critical for notation, formulas, and understanding inference.

Concept Sample (The group we have) Population (The group we want to know about)
Definition A subset of the population (e.g., 200 students surveyed). The entire group of interest (e.g., All college students in the US).
Mean Symbol M (or x̄) μ (Mu)
Std Dev Symbol SD (or s) σ (Sigma)
What we do with it Calculate statistics to estimate population parameters. The "Truth" we want to know (usually unknown).

The Sampling Process

For our sample to tell us something useful about the population, we ideally need random sampling. But in practice, researchers use different sampling methods.

Types of Samples

Sample Type Description Example
Random Sample Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected. The gold standard for generalizability. Using a random number generator to select students from a complete list of all university students.
Convenience Sample Participants selected because they are easy to access. Most common in research. A professor surveys students in their own class.
Volunteer Sample Participants self-select to participate. May introduce bias (volunteers differ from non-volunteers). People who post reviews on RateMyProfessor.com or respond to online surveys.
Cluster Sample Population divided into groups (clusters), then entire clusters are randomly selected. Randomly selecting 10 schools, then surveying all students in those schools.
Why are true random samples rarely used? Researchers rarely have access to the entire population. You can't randomly sample "all college students in the US" if you don't have a list of every single one. This is why convenience and volunteer samples are more common — but they limit how well results generalize.

Random Sampling vs. Random Assignment

These are two different concepts that students often confuse:

  • Random Sampling (Random Selection): How you SELECT participants from the population. Affects generalizability (can you apply results to the whole population?).
  • Random Assignment: How you ASSIGN participants to experimental conditions (e.g., treatment vs. control). Affects internal validity (can you conclude causation?).

Example: Mark flips a coin to determine which condition each participant goes into. This is random assignment (not random sampling — the participants weren't randomly selected from a population).

Control Group vs. Experimental Group

In experiments, participants are assigned to different conditions:

  • Control Group: The group that does NOT receive the treatment. They serve as a baseline for comparison. They might receive a placebo or no intervention.
  • Experimental Group: The group that DOES receive the treatment or intervention being tested.

Why it matters: Without a control group, you can't know if changes are due to the treatment or other factors (time, placebo effect, etc.).

Knowledge Check: Random Assignment

A researcher wants to test if a new study method improves exam scores. She recruits 60 volunteers from her class. She flips a coin for each student: heads = new study method, tails = traditional method.

Is this random sampling or random assignment?

Random Assignment.

The coin flip determines which GROUP each participant goes into (experimental vs. control). This is random assignment.

It is NOT random sampling because the participants were not randomly selected from a population — they were volunteers from her class (a convenience sample).

Probability Basics

Probability is the mathematical language that connects samples to populations. It quantifies how likely an event is to occur.

p = Target Outcomes / All Possible Outcomes

Rules of Probability:

  • Probability is expressed as a decimal (0.00 to 1.00).
  • 0 = Impossible, 1 = Certain.
  • Do not use percentages (50%) or fractions (1/2) for your final answer in this course. Use 0.50.

Practice: The Dice Roll

You roll a standard 6-sided die. What is the probability of getting an even number?

Total Outcomes: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 (N=6)
Target Outcomes (Even): 2, 4, 6 (N=3)

Calculation: p = 3 / 6 = 0.5

Independent vs. Dependent Probability

Events can be independent or dependent — this affects how we calculate combined probabilities.

Type Definition Example
Independent Events The outcome of one event does NOT affect the probability of the other event. Flipping a coin twice. Getting heads on flip 1 doesn't change the probability of heads on flip 2.
Dependent Events The outcome of one event DOES affect the probability of the other event. Drawing cards without replacement. If you draw an Ace first, there are fewer Aces left for the second draw.

Example: Independent Probability

Question: You flip a fair coin twice. What is the probability of getting heads both times?

Why Independent? The result of the first flip has no effect on the second flip. Each flip is a separate, independent event.

Calculation: p(Heads AND Heads) = p(Heads) × p(Heads) = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25

Knowledge Check: Independence

A bag contains 4 red and 6 blue marbles. You draw one marble, put it back, then draw again.

Are these draws independent or dependent events?

Independent.

Because you put the marble back ("with replacement"), the probabilities stay the same for the second draw. If you did NOT put it back, they would be dependent events.

Connecting Probability to Sampling: When we randomly select someone from a population, each person has a known probability of being selected. This is why random sampling is so powerful — it allows us to use probability math to determine how likely our sample results are to reflect the true population.

Knowledge Check

A bag contains 2 Red balls, 3 Blue balls, and 5 Green balls. What is the probability of drawing a Green ball?

p = 0.5

Total balls = 2 + 3 + 5 = 10.

Target (Green) = 5.

p = 5 / 10 = 0.5.

The Normal Distribution & Z-Scores

The Normal Distribution

Many natural phenomena follow a Normal Distribution (also called the "bell curve"): height, IQ, blood pressure, test scores, etc.

Key Properties of the Normal Distribution:
  • Symmetrical: The left and right sides are mirror images.
  • Mean = Median = Mode: All three measures of central tendency are at the center. This means: if μ = 151, then the median is also 151!
  • Predictable Areas: We know exactly what percentage of scores fall within any range.

The 68-95-99.7 Rule (Empirical Rule)

In any normal distribution:

  • 68% of scores fall within 1 SD of the mean (between Z = -1 and Z = +1)
  • 95% of scores fall within 2 SDs of the mean (between Z = -2 and Z = +2)
  • 99.7% of scores fall within 3 SDs of the mean (between Z = -3 and Z = +3)

This is why Z-scores beyond ±3 are extremely rare!

The Z-Score: Your Location on the Curve

How do you compare two different things? For example, a teacher's salary in US Dollars vs. Japanese Yen? You can't compare the raw numbers directly. You must standardize them.

A Z-score tells you exactly where a score lies in a distribution by counting how many Standard Deviations (SD) it is away from the Mean.

Z = (X - μ) / σ
The Standard Normal (Z) Distribution: When we convert all raw scores to Z-scores, we create a Z distribution with these properties:
  • Mean = 0 (because the average person is 0 SDs from the mean)
  • Standard Deviation = 1 (by definition, Z measures distance in SD units)
This is why the Z distribution is also called the "standard normal distribution."

Where X is the raw score, μ is the mean, and σ is the standard deviation.

Interpreting Z-Scores

  • Z = 0: The score is exactly the average.
  • Z = +1.0: The score is 1 SD above the average.
  • Z = -2.5: The score is 2.5 SDs below the average (very low).
Which Z-score is more "extreme"? When comparing how far scores are from the mean, look at the absolute value of the Z-score (ignore the sign).
  • Z = +0.68 is 0.68 SDs from the mean
  • Z = -0.92 is 0.92 SDs from the mean
So Z = -0.92 is more extreme because |−0.92| > |0.68|. The negative sign just means it's below the mean, not that it's less extreme.

Knowledge Check: Extreme Z-Scores

Which of these Z-scores represents a score that is MOST different from the mean?

  • A) Z = +1.25
  • B) Z = -1.50
  • C) Z = +0.90
  • D) Z = -0.75

B) Z = -1.50

Compare absolute values: |+1.25| = 1.25, |-1.50| = 1.50, |+0.90| = 0.90, |-0.75| = 0.75

1.50 is the largest, so Z = -1.50 is most extreme (furthest from the mean). The negative sign indicates it's below average, but doesn't make it "less extreme."

Scenario: Who did better?

Teacher A (Arizona): Salary = $50,000 (Mean = $32,000, SD = $20,000)

Teacher B (Japan): Salary = ¥4,000,000 (Mean = ¥2,500,000, SD = ¥1,200,000)

Step 1: Calculate Z for Teacher A
Z = (50,000 - 32,000) / 20,000 = 18,000 / 20,000 = +0.90

Step 2: Calculate Z for Teacher B
Z = (4,000,000 - 2,500,000) / 1,200,000 = 1,500,000 / 1,200,000 = +1.25

Conclusion: Teacher B is doing better relative to their local economy (1.25 SDs above average vs 0.90 SDs).

Common Pitfall: Order of Operations! You must subtract the Mean from the Score first, then divide.
Correct: (100 - 80) / 10 = 2
Incorrect: 100 - 80 / 10 = 92 (Calculator error)

Knowledge Check

Mean = 50, SD = 5. Your score (X) is 45. What is your Z-score?

Z = -1.0

Z = (45 - 50) / 5

Z = -5 / 5 = -1.0

(The negative sign is crucial! It means you are below the mean.)

Mastering the Z-Table

The Z-table connects Scores to Probabilities (Percentages). This is the most technical skill in Module 2.

The Golden Rule of Symmetry: The Normal Curve is symmetrical.
The area above Z = +1.0 is the exact same as the area below Z = -1.0.
If your Z-table doesn't show negative numbers, just look up the positive version!

Direction 1: Have Z → Find % (Probability)

Use this when asking: "What percentage of people scored higher/lower than X?"

  1. Calculate Z score.
  2. Look up Z in the table (e.g., 1.50).
  3. Find the "Area in Tail" (if asking for the extreme) or "Area between Mean and Z".

Example

Z = +1.5. What percentage of people scored higher than this?

1. Look up 1.5 in the Z-table.

2. "Area in Tail" = 0.0668 (or 6.68%).

3. Answer: 6.68% of people scored higher.

Direction 2: Have % → Find Z (Cut-off Scores)

Use this when asking: "What score do I need to be in the top 10%?" or "What is the 90th percentile?"

  1. Determine the area in the tail. (If top 10%, tail = 0.10).
  2. Look inside the body of the table for the number closest to 0.1000.
  3. Trace back to find the Z-score.
  4. Convert Z back to Raw Score using the formula below.

Converting Z to Raw Score:

X = μ + (Z)(σ)

Rearranged from Z = (X - μ) / σ. Just solve for X!

Common Critical Z-Values (Memorize These!)

Tail Area One-Tailed Z Two-Tailed Z (±) Common Use
0.10 (10%) ±1.28 ±1.645 Top/Bottom 10%
0.05 (5%) ±1.645 ±1.96 Top/Bottom 5%; 95% CI
0.025 (2.5%) ±1.96 — 97.5th percentile
0.01 (1%) ±2.33 ±2.58 Top/Bottom 1%
Assignment Helper (Q3/Q4):

Scenario: "Identify the top 5% of students."

1. Area in tail = 0.05.

2. Look inside the table for 0.0500. (It's usually between 0.0495 and 0.0505).

3. The Z-score is 1.645.

4. Since it's the "Top" 5%, Z is positive (+1.645). If it were "Bottom" 5%, Z would be negative (-1.645).

Knowledge Check

You want to find the 97.5th percentile. This leaves 2.5% (0.0250) in the top tail. Look at a Z-table (or recall the lecture). What is the Z-score?

Z = 1.96

This is a famous number in statistics! The area in the tail for Z=1.96 is exactly 0.0250.

The Sampling Distribution: The Bridge to Inference

This is the most important concept in Module 2. It explains how we can use one sample to make conclusions about an entire population.

The Big Picture Problem

Imagine: You want to know the average IQ of all college students in the US (the population mean, μ).

You can't test every student. So you test 100 students and get a sample mean (M) of 105.

The Question: How confident can you be that 105 is close to the true population mean?

What Is a Sampling Distribution?

Imagine you could repeat your study thousands of times:

  1. Take a random sample of 100 students → calculate the mean (e.g., 105)
  2. Take another random sample of 100 → calculate the mean (e.g., 98)
  3. Take another sample → mean = 102
  4. ...repeat this thousands of times...

If you plotted all these sample means, you would get the Sampling Distribution of the Mean — a distribution of all possible sample means.

Terminology Note

These terms all mean the same thing:

  • Sampling Distribution
  • Sampling Distribution of the Mean
  • Distribution of Means
  • Distribution of Sample Means

If you see any of these on an exam, they refer to the same concept: the theoretical distribution of all possible sample means.

Key Insight: The sampling distribution is NOT your data. It's a theoretical distribution of what could happen if you took many samples. It tells us how much sample means naturally vary due to random chance.

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

This is one of the most powerful theorems in statistics. It says:

No matter the shape of the population distribution,

the sampling distribution of sample means will be approximately Normal

(as long as sample size is large enough, typically n ≥ 30)

Why this matters: Because the sampling distribution is Normal, we can use Z-scores and the Z-table to calculate probabilities about sample means!

Standard Error: How Much Do Sample Means Vary?

Just like individual scores have a standard deviation (σ), sample means have their own measure of spread called the Standard Error (SE).

SE = σ / √n

Where σ is the population standard deviation and n is the sample size.

Concept Standard Deviation (σ) Standard Error (SE)
Measures spread of... Individual scores in a population Sample means across many samples
Example "How much do individual IQ scores vary?" "How much do sample means vary from sample to sample?"
Critical Insight: Notice the √n in the denominator. As your sample size increases, the Standard Error decreases. This means:
  • Larger samples → less variability in sample means → more precision
  • With n=100, SE = σ/10 (one-tenth of the SD)
  • This is why larger samples give more reliable estimates!
Key Exam Concept: The distribution of sample means (sampling distribution) always has LOWER variance than the distribution of individual scores. Why? Because sample means average out individual variation. A single person might be extremely tall or short, but if you average 50 people, extreme values cancel out.

Example: Calculating Standard Error

Given: Population mean (μ) = 100, Population SD (σ) = 15, Sample size (n) = 25

Calculate SE:

SE = σ / √n = 15 / √25 = 15 / 5 = 3

Interpretation: While individual IQ scores vary by about 15 points (σ), sample means of 25 people only vary by about 3 points. The sampling distribution is much "tighter" than the original distribution!

Quick Practice: Standard Error

A population has μ = 100 and σ = 15. If you take samples of n = 20, what is the standard error of the sampling distribution?

SE = 15 / √20 = 15 / 4.47 = 3.35

Remember: √20 ≈ 4.47. The standard error tells us how much sample means will typically vary from the true population mean.

Connecting It All: Z-Scores for Sample Means

When working with sample means instead of individual scores, we modify the Z-score formula:

Z = (M - μ) / SE = (M - μ) / (σ / √n)

Where M is the sample mean, μ is the population mean, and SE is the standard error.

Knowledge Check

Population: μ = 500, σ = 100. You take a sample of n = 64 and find M = 520.

1. What is the Standard Error?

2. What is the Z-score for this sample mean?

1. SE = 100 / √64 = 100 / 8 = 12.5

2. Z = (520 - 500) / 12.5 = 20 / 12.5 = 1.6

This sample mean is 1.6 standard errors above the population mean. Using the Z-table, this is a relatively unusual result (only about 5.5% of samples would have a mean this high or higher).

The Logic of Hypothesis Testing

Now we connect everything: samples, probability, Z-scores, and the sampling distribution all come together in hypothesis testing.

The Connection to Sampling Distribution: We know from Tab 4 that sample means vary predictably (following a normal distribution with standard error). This allows us to ask: "If the Null Hypothesis is true, how likely is it that we'd get a sample mean this extreme?" That probability is the p-value.

We want to prove our idea (Research Hypothesis), but math can't prove things true. It can only prove things unlikely.

The Courtroom Analogy

  • Null Hypothesis ($H_0$): The defendant is Innocent. (There is NO effect. The drug does nothing).
  • Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$): The defendant is Guilty. (There IS an effect. The drug works).
Terminology Note: The Alternative Hypothesis is also called the Research Hypothesis — these terms mean the same thing. It's what the researcher believes or hopes to find evidence for.

The Process: We assume the Null ($H_0$) is true. We collect evidence (Data). If the evidence is overwhelmingly unlikely to happen if the defendant were innocent, we Reject the Null.

Key Decision Rule: When your results support your research hypothesis (alternative hypothesis), you reject the null hypothesis. You never "accept" the alternative — you reject the null based on statistical evidence.

The "P-Value" and Alpha ($\alpha$)

  • Alpha ($\alpha$): The "Reasonable Doubt" threshold. Usually set at 0.05 (5%).
  • p-value: The probability that your result happened by random luck.

If p < α (p is low):

REJECT the Null Hypothesis.

(The result is Statistically Significant)

If p ≥ α (p is high):

FAIL TO REJECT the Null Hypothesis.

(The result is NOT Statistically Significant)

Scenario: The SAT Prep Course

$H_0$: The Prep Course has NO effect. ($\mu_{prep} = \mu_{general}$)

Result: We tested a student, "Student B". The probability of Student B getting their score by random luck was 0.03 (3%).

Decision: Since 0.03 < 0.05, we Reject the Null. It is too unlikely that Student B got this score by luck. The Prep Course likely works.

Tails and Errors

One-Tailed vs. Two-Tailed Tests

Type Two-Tailed (Non-Directional) One-Tailed (Directional)
Meaning We just want to see a difference (Higher OR Lower). We predict a specific direction (Higher ONLY or Lower ONLY).
Alpha Split 0.05 is split into two tails (0.025 on left, 0.025 on right). 0.05 is put entirely into one tail.
Critical Z ±1.96 (Harder to reach) +1.645 (Easier to reach)
Usage The Default. Use unless you have a very strong reason. Only if you don't care about the opposite result.

Type I and Type II Errors

We are making guesses based on probability, so we might be wrong.

Type I Error (False Positive)

Definition: You Rejected the Null, but you shouldn't have.

In plain English: You said there was an effect (e.g., "The drug works!"), but there actually wasn't. It was just a fluke.

Controlled by: Alpha level ($\alpha$).

Type II Error (False Negative)

Definition: You Failed to Reject the Null, but you should have.

In plain English: You missed it. There really was an effect, but your study didn't catch it.

Type I Error and Replication

Scenario: A researcher publishes a study claiming a new supplement improves memory. However, when other researchers try to replicate the study, they cannot reproduce the results.

What likely happened? The original finding was probably a Type I Error. The researcher found a "significant" result by chance when no real effect existed. This is why replication is so important in science!

The Importance of Replication

Replication means repeating a study to see if results can be reproduced. It's one of the most important safeguards against Type I errors.

Type of Replication Description What It Tests
Direct Replication Repeat the exact same study with the same methods. Was the original finding real or a fluke?
Conceptual Replication Test the same idea but with different methods or measures. Does the effect generalize beyond one specific method?
Replication in New Contexts Test the finding in a different population, culture, or setting. Does the effect generalize to other groups? (Tests external validity)
Why Replicate in New Contexts? A finding from American college students might not apply to older adults, people from other cultures, or clinical populations. Replicating in new contexts tests the external validity — how broadly the results generalize.

Knowledge Check

A researcher claims a new teaching method improves grades. In reality, the method does nothing, but by random chance, the sample group got high grades and the researcher published the finding. What type of error is this?

Type I Error (False Positive). The researcher found a "significant" result when one did not truly exist in the population.

Knowledge Check: Replication

A study found that a meditation app reduces anxiety in college students. Researchers want to see if this effect also occurs among elderly adults in retirement homes.

What type of replication is this?

Replication in a new context.

They are testing whether the finding generalizes to a different population (elderly adults vs. college students). This tests external validity.

Q3 & Q4: The Gifted Program (Tricky!)

This asks you to go from % → Z → Raw Score.

Step-by-Step Guide for "Top 4% Cutoff"

1. Find the Area in Body: Top 4% means 0.04 in the tail. Look up 0.0400 in the tail column of your Z-table (or 0.9600 in the body).
→ Closest Z is approx 1.75.

2. Convert Z to Raw Score:
Formula: X = μ + (Z)(σ)
If Mean=100 and SD=15:
X = 100 + (1.75 * 15) = 100 + 26.25 = 126.25.

3. Conclusion: A student needs a score of 126.25 to be in the top 4%.

Percentile Logic:
96th Percentile = Top 4% (Z will be positive).
5th Percentile = Bottom 5% (Z will be negative).

Q5: Two-Tailed Cutoffs (Top AND Bottom)

Sometimes you need to find cutoffs for BOTH ends of the distribution. This is common for identifying people who are extremely high OR extremely low.

Practice: Two-Tailed Cutoffs

Scenario: A health program wants to identify patients with extreme blood pressure (either very high or very low). They want to flag the top 3% and bottom 3%.

Given: Mean BP = 120 mmHg, SD = 15 mmHg

Find both cutoff scores.

Step 1: Find Z for 3% in the tail.

Look up 0.0300 in the tail column of the Z-table. The closest Z ≈ 1.88.

Step 2: Calculate BOTH cutoffs.

Top 3% (High BP): Z = +1.88
X = μ + (Z)(σ) = 120 + (1.88)(15) = 120 + 28.2 = 148.2 mmHg

Bottom 3% (Low BP): Z = -1.88 (same number, negative)
X = μ + (Z)(σ) = 120 + (-1.88)(15) = 120 - 28.2 = 91.8 mmHg

Conclusion: Patients with BP above 148.2 or below 91.8 would be flagged.

Key Insight for Two-Tailed Problems: The Z-value is the SAME for both tails (just opposite signs). If top 3% uses Z = +1.88, then bottom 3% uses Z = -1.88.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake How to Fix It
Using percentage instead of decimal (writing 0.50% instead of 0.50) Always express probability as a decimal between 0 and 1
Forgetting the negative sign for below-average Z-scores If X < μ, the Z-score MUST be negative
Using SD instead of SE when working with sample means For individual scores: use σ. For sample means: use SE = σ/√n
Confusing "top X%" with "Xth percentile" Top 5% = 95th percentile (the highest scores). Bottom 5% = 5th percentile.
