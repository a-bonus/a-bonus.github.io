The Logic of Variables

Before calculating anything, you must identify what you are studying. In research, we look for relationships between variables.

Independent (IV) vs. Dependent (DV)

Use the sentence: "Does [Variable A] depend on [Variable B]?"

  • The variable that "depends" is the Dependent Variable (DV) (The Outcome).
  • The variable that influences the other is the Independent Variable (IV) (The Grouper/Predictor).

Practice Scenario: Coffee & Sleep

Research Question: Does drinking coffee affect how many hours a student sleeps?

Ask yourself: Does coffee consumption depend on sleep? Or does sleep depend on coffee consumption?

DV (Outcome): Hours of Sleep. (This is what we measure to see the effect).

IV (Predictor): Coffee Consumption.

Variables to analyze: Coffee Consumption (IV) and Hours of Sleep (DV).

Demographic Splits

Often, a researcher wants to compare groups (e.g., "Do Seniors sleep more than Freshmen?").

  • Variable 1: Class Standing (Freshman, Senior). This is the grouping variable.
  • Variable 2: Sleep Hours. This is the measured variable.
  • Note: "Freshman" is not a variable. "Freshman" is a level or category of the variable "Class Standing."

Naming & Measuring Variables

1. Naming Convention

Name the category, not the specific answer.

  • ❌ Variable: "Yes"
  • ✅ Variable: "Response" (Levels: Yes, No)
  • ❌ Variable: "Male"
  • ✅ Variable: "Gender" (Levels: Male, Female, Non-binary)

2. Scales of Measurement

Identifying the scale tells you which Excel formula to use.

Type Key Feature Examples
Nominal Names/Labels. No math possible. Computer Type (Mac/PC), Time Zone, Employment Status.
Ordinal Ranked Order. Distance unknown. Class Standing (Freshman < Sophomore), Single Likert item (1-5).
Scale / Equal-Interval Real numbers. Math works. Age, Time (minutes), Height.
⚠️ Important Exception: Composite Scores
If a researcher takes 10 survey questions (Likert scale 1-7) and averages them to create a "Total Satisfaction Score," that new score is treated as Scale/Interval, not Ordinal.

Descriptive Statistics

When summarizing data, you need to find the center (Central Tendency) and the spread (Variability).

Choosing the Right Measure

  • Mean (Average): Use for Scale data that is symmetrical (Normal Distribution).
    Sensitive to outliers!
  • Median (Middle): Use for Scale data that is Skewed (has extreme outliers).
    Example: Housing prices, Salaries.
  • Mode (Most Frequent): Use for Nominal/Categorical data.
    Example: The most common computer type.

Calculating Variance & Standard Deviation (Process)

Your assignment may ask you to show the "Calculation Process." Do not just write the answer.

Example: Showing Your Work

Data: 2, 4, 6

Step 1: Mean (M) = (2+4+6)/3 = 4

Step 2: Deviations (X - M)
2 - 4 = -2
4 - 4 = 0
6 - 4 = +2

Step 3: Squared Deviations (SS)
-2² = 4
0² = 0
2² = 4
Sum of Squares = 8

Step 4: Variance (SS / N-1)
8 / (3-1) = 4

Step 5: Standard Deviation (√Variance)
√4 = 2

Visuals & APA Formatting

Graph Selection Guide

Bar Graph

Use for: Nominal/Categorical Data.

Visual: Bars DO NOT touch.

Why: Categories are distinct (Mac vs PC).

Histogram

Use for: Scale/Continuous Data.

Visual: Bars TOUCH.

Why: Data flows (Age 20 is next to 21).

APA Table Rules (The "Clean Look")

APA tables are minimalist. Follow these strict rules to avoid point deductions:

Table 1
Descriptive Title Goes Here in Italics
Category Frequency Percentage
Category A 10 50%
Category B 10 50%
Total 20 100%
  • ✅ Horizontal Lines: Only at the top, under headers, and at the very bottom.
  • ❌ Vertical Lines: NEVER use vertical lines to separate columns.
  • ✅ Title: Above the table, Italicized.

APA Figure Rules

  • Label: "Figure 1" goes BELOW the graph.
  • Caption: A descriptive sentence goes below the label.
  • Axes: Both X and Y axes must be labeled clearly (e.g., "Age Groups" not just "1, 2, 3").

Excel Workshop

Assignment Tip: You may need to calculate stats for only one specific group (e.g., "Unemployed Students"). You cannot run the formula on the whole column! You must Sort first.

1. How to Sort & Filter Data

  1. Highlight all your data (including headers).
  2. Go to the Data tab > Click Sort.
  3. Sort by the category you need (e.g., "Employment Status").
  4. Now, look at the rows. All "Unemployed" students will be grouped together (e.g., Rows 2 through 15).
  5. Run your formulas only on those specific cells (e.g., =AVERAGE(B2:B15)).

2. Formulas Cheat Sheet

Mean: =AVERAGE(range)
Median: =MEDIAN(range)
Mode: =MODE.SNGL(range)
Variance (Sample): =VAR.S(range)
Std Dev (Sample): =STDEV.S(range)
Range: =MAX(range) - MIN(range)

3. Creating a Grouped Frequency Table (Histograms)

If you have a wide range of data (like Age 18-60), you cannot list every single age. You must create Bins (Groups).

  1. Decide your bins (e.g., 20-29, 30-39).
  2. In Excel, verify your count.
  3. Rule of Thumb: Aim for 5 to 10 bins. Too few = no detail. Too many = messy.
  4. Ensure your histogram uses these bins on the X-axis.
