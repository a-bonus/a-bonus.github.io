The Logic of the Z-Test

A Z-test compares a Sample Mean (M) to a known Population Mean (Œº). We usually know the population mean and standard deviation from historical data (e.g., Census data, National averages).

1. Identifying Variables (IV vs. DV)

Before you calculate anything, you must know who is who. This is Question 1 on your Quiz.

The "Chandler vs. Neighborhood" Example

Scenario: You want to know if household sizes in your neighborhood differ from the City of Chandler.

  • Independent Variable (IV): The "Grouper." What separates the two groups?
    Answer: Location (Chandler vs. Neighborhood).
  • Dependent Variable (DV): The "Measurement." What numbers are we averaging?
    Answer: Household Size.

2. Writing Hypotheses

You must write two hypotheses for every test.

Type Definition Key Symbols
Null Hypothesis ($H_0$) The "Boring" Hypothesis. Assumes there is NO difference. Any difference found is just luck/chance. Always includes the Equals (=) sign.
($=$, $\le$, $\ge$)
Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$) Also called the "Research Hypothesis." Assumes there IS a difference. This is usually what you want to prove. Never includes equals.
($\neq$, $<$, $>$)
Terminology Note: "Research Hypothesis" is just another name for the Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$). They mean the same thing. If you see "research hypothesis" on a quiz, think $H_1$.

3. One-Tailed vs. Two-Tailed

This determines how you write your symbols and find your critical values.

Two-Tailed (Non-Directional)

Keywords: "Is there a difference?", "Differ from"

Logic: I don't know if it will be higher or lower, just different.

Symbols:
$H_0: \mu_{sample} = \mu_{pop}$
$H_1: \mu_{sample} \neq \mu_{pop}$

One-Tailed (Directional)

Keywords: "Higher", "Lower", "Better", "Worse", "Increase"

Logic: I predict a specific direction.

Symbols (e.g., Higher):
$H_0: \mu_{sample} \le \mu_{pop}$ (Note the opposite coverage)
$H_1: \mu_{sample} > \mu_{pop}$

Quiz Practice: Question 2

Scenario: I am interested in examining any difference between my neighborhood and the city. I am not predicting any particular direction.

What is the correct Null Hypothesis?

"Household sizes in my neighborhood are not significantly different from the general household sizes in the whole city of Chandler."

Because it is non-directional, the Null says "No Difference."

4. Two-Population Notation ($\mu_1$ vs $\mu_2$)

When comparing two groups (not a sample to a known population), we use subscript notation.

Example: Memory Study

Scenario: Researchers hypothesize that participants in a stress condition will perform worse than participants in a control group on a memory test.

Let Population 1 = Stress group, Population 2 = Control group.

Because stress is predicted to cause WORSE performance:

$H_0: \mu_1 \ge \mu_2$ (Stress group is the same or better)

$H_1: \mu_1 < \mu_2$ (Stress group performs worse = lower scores)

Example: Women Talk More?

Scenario: Testing if women (Population 1) talk more than men (Population 2).

Because we predict women talk MORE:

$H_0: \mu_1 \le \mu_2$ (Women talk the same or less)

$H_1: \mu_1 > \mu_2$ (Women talk more)

Quick Rule: The alternative hypothesis ($H_1$) always matches your prediction. If you predict Group 1 is higher, use $\mu_1 > \mu_2$. If you predict Group 1 is lower, use $\mu_1 < \mu_2$.

Quiz Practice: Two-Group Comparison

Scenario: Researchers were interested in whether playing Dance Dance Revolution affected the heart rate of overweight adolescents differently than non-overweight adolescents. Both groups played DDR while researchers measured heart rate.

What is the null hypothesis?

"There will be no difference in the average heart rates of the two groups after both groups play Dance Dance Revolution."

The null hypothesis always says "no difference" or "no effect." Since the researchers are just asking if the groups differ (not which is higher), this is non-directional.

The Calculation Engine

To perform a Z-test, you need to calculate two numbers in a specific order: Standard Error (SE) and the Z-statistic.

Requirements for a Z-Test (Parametric Test)

Important: A Z-test is a parametric test, which means it has requirements:
  • The Dependent Variable (DV) must be a scale variable (interval or ratio level of measurement)
  • Examples of scale variables: IQ scores, height, weight, test scores, income
  • You cannot use a Z-test with nominal (categories) or ordinal (rankings) data

What is the Z-Table Used For?

The Z-table serves several purposes:

  • Converting a Z-score to a percentile: Find what percentage of scores fall below a given Z
  • Converting a raw score to a percentile: First calculate Z, then look up the percentage
  • Finding critical values: Determine the Z cutoffs for hypothesis testing
Quiz Tip: The Z-table is primarily used for converting a raw score to a percentile score. You first convert the raw score to a Z-score, then use the table to find the corresponding percentile.
Crucial Concept: In Module 2, we used Standard Deviation ($\sigma$) for single scores. Now that we are testing a Sample Mean, we must use Standard Error ($SE$).

Step 1: Calculate the Sample Mean ($M$)

Add up all your scores and divide by $N$ (number of scores).

$M = \frac{\sum X}{N}$

Step 2: Calculate Standard Error ($SE$)

This tells us how much sample means typically vary from the population mean. You need the Population SD ($\sigma$) and Sample Size ($N$).

$SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}$

Tip: Do this calculation first! You cannot find Z without it.
Excel Formula: =PopulationSD / SQRT(N)

Step 3: Calculate the Z-Statistic

This places your sample on the curve. It tells you how many "Standard Errors" away from the population mean your sample is.

$Z = \frac{M - \mu}{SE}$

Guided Practice: The "Chandler" Numbers

Given:
Population Mean ($\mu$) = 3.1
Population SD ($\sigma$) = 1.6
Sample Size ($N$) = 16
Sample Mean ($M$) = 3.5 (Calculated from list)

1. Calculate SE:

$SE = 1.6 / \sqrt{16}$

$SE = 1.6 / 4$

$SE = 0.4$

2. Calculate Z-Statistic:

$Z = (3.5 - 3.1) / 0.4$

$Z = 0.4 / 0.4$

$Z = 1.0$

Making the Decision

Now that you have a Z-statistic (e.g., 1.0), is that "significant"? Is it weird enough to reject the Null?

1. Determine Tails & Alpha

  • Alpha ($\alpha$): Usually 0.05 or 0.01. This is your "risk threshold" for error.
  • Two-Tailed: If your hypothesis was "difference" (non-directional). Split alpha into two tails.
  • One-Tailed: If your hypothesis was "higher/lower" (directional). Put all alpha in one tail.

2. Find the Critical Z Value (The "Cutoff")

Use your Z-table or memorize these common cutoffs. These are the numbers your Calculated Z must beat.

Alpha Level ($\alpha$) Test Type Critical Z Values
0.05 Two-Tailed ¬±1.96 (Most Common)
0.05 One-Tailed ¬±1.645
0.01 Two-Tailed ¬±2.58
0.01 One-Tailed ¬±2.33
0.10 Two-Tailed ¬±1.645
0.10 One-Tailed ¬±1.28
How to find critical Z values: Look up the alpha level in the Z-table. For two-tailed tests, split alpha in half (e.g., $\alpha = .10$ ‚Üí look for .05 in each tail). For one-tailed tests, use the full alpha value.
Quiz Question 7 Hint: The Chandler quiz asks for $\alpha = 0.10$ (Two-Tailed). Look at the table above. The cutoff is ¬±1.645.

3. Compare and Decide

The Rule: If your Calculated Z is MORE EXTREME (further from zero) than the Critical Z, you REJECT the Null.

Chandler Decision Practice

Calculated Z: 1.0
Critical Z (at $\alpha = .10$, 2-tail): ¬±1.645

Comparison: Is 1.0 more extreme than 1.645?

No. 1.0 is closer to zero. It is inside the "safe zone."

Decision: Fail to Reject the Null Hypothesis.

Conclusion in English: "The household sizes in my neighborhood are not significantly different from the city of Chandler."

Interpreting "Fail to Reject": It doesn't mean the Null is true. It just means we didn't have enough evidence to prove it false. It's like a "Not Guilty" verdict in court (which is not the same as "Innocent").

5. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Confusing SE and œÉ
  • œÉ (Population SD): Use for individual scores and Cohen's d
  • SE (Standard Error): Use for Z-statistic when testing sample means

Remember: SE = œÉ / ‚àöN. SE is always smaller than œÉ.

Mistake #2: Wrong denominator in Cohen's d
  • Z-statistic: $Z = (M - \mu) / \mathbf{SE}$
  • Cohen's d: $d = (M - \mu) / \mathbf{\sigma}$

Cohen's d uses the population SD, NOT the standard error!

Mistake #3: Using Œ±=.05 Two-Tailed values for One-Tailed tests
  • Two-tailed at Œ±=.05: Critical Z = ¬±1.96
  • One-tailed at Œ±=.05: Critical Z = ¬±1.645

Always check: Is the hypothesis directional or non-directional?

Mistake #4: Saying "Accept the Null"

We never "accept" the null hypothesis. We either:

  • Reject the null (evidence against it)
  • Fail to reject the null (insufficient evidence)

Effect Size & Power

Significance tells us "Yes/No." Effect Size tells us "How much?" Power tells us "Did we miss it?"

1. Effect Size (Cohen's d)

Even if a result is not significant (like our Chandler example), we often calculate Effect Size. It measures the difference between means in standard deviation units.

$d = \frac{M - \mu}{\sigma}$
‚ö†Ô∏è MAJOR TRAP ALERT ‚ö†Ô∏è
Notice the denominator? It is $\sigma$ (Population SD), NOT $SE$.
Z-statistic uses SE. Cohen's d uses $\sigma$.
Why? Effect size is about the populations, not the sample size.

Chandler Effect Size

$M = 3.5$, $\mu = 3.1$, $\sigma = 1.6$

$d = (3.5 - 3.1) / 1.6$

$d = 0.4 / 1.6 = \mathbf{0.25}$

Interpretation: This is a Small effect. (0.2=Small, 0.5=Medium, 0.8=Large).

Another Example: Donut Calories

Scenario: Krispy Kreme donuts average 260 calories ($\mu = 260$) with SD = 35. A local bakery's donuts average 240 calories.

$d = (240 - 260) / 35 = -20 / 35 = \mathbf{-0.57}$

Interpretation: This is a Medium effect. The negative sign indicates the local bakery has fewer calories.

2. Statistical Power

Power is the ability to correctly reject a false Null Hypothesis (i.e., finding an effect when it really exists).

Concept Definition Probability Symbol
Type I Error False Alarm. Rejecting Null when it's actually True. $\alpha$ (Alpha)
Type II Error Missed Discovery. Failing to Reject Null when it's False. $\beta$ (Beta)
Power Correct Hit. Rejecting Null when it is indeed False. $1 - \beta$

Interpreting Power Values

Example: Power = 0.93

What does this mean?

If the null hypothesis is FALSE (there really is an effect), there is a 93% chance of rejecting the null hypothesis (correctly detecting the effect).

Conversely, there is a 7% chance of missing it (Type II error, $\beta = 0.07$).

The 80% Power Convention:
Researchers generally want power of at least 0.80 (80%). If power is less than 80%, the study has insufficient power and there's too high a risk of missing a real effect.

The Practical Use of Power

Power analysis tells researchers how many participants are needed to conduct a study with findings they can trust. Before running a study, researchers calculate the required sample size to achieve at least 80% power.

How to Increase Power?

  1. Increase Sample Size (N): This is the easiest way. It lowers Standard Error, making Z larger.
  2. Increase Alpha ($\alpha$): Raising alpha from .01 to .05 makes it easier to reject (but increases Type I error risk).
  3. Increase Effect Size: Using a stronger treatment.

3. The Alpha-Power-Error Relationship

Alpha ($\alpha$) and Power are connected in important ways:

Key Relationship: When alpha increases, both the probability of Type I error AND statistical power increase.
  • Higher $\alpha$ (e.g., .10 instead of .05) ‚Üí Easier to reject null ‚Üí More power
  • But also ‚Üí More risk of false alarms (Type I errors)

The typical probability (alpha) researchers use is 0.05.

4. Point Estimates & Confidence Intervals

When reporting results, we use three key terms:

Term Definition Example
Point Estimate A single value (usually the sample mean or effect size) that represents our best guess. If exposure to tears lowered arousal by 1.27 points, the point estimate is 1.27
Margin of Error The range of uncertainty around the point estimate. ¬± 0.32 points
Confidence Interval The range created by point estimate ¬± margin of error. [0.95, 1.59] or written as 1.27 ¬± 0.32

5. Meta-Analysis & Confidence Intervals

A meta-analysis combines results from multiple studies to get an overall effect size. When interpreting meta-analysis results:

How to Interpret Meta-Analysis Results

Example: Mean $d = 0.11$ with 95% CI of (-0.04, 0.26)

Key Question: Does the confidence interval include zero?

  • If the CI includes zero (like -0.04 to 0.26): The effect is NOT statistically significant. We cannot conclude there is a real effect.
  • If the CI does NOT include zero: The effect IS statistically significant.

Answer for this example: Averaging across all of the literature, there really is no effect (because the CI crosses zero).

Quick Quiz: Test Your Understanding

Q1: What is the alpha level used for in a Z-test?

Alpha is used to determine the critical Z value(s) marking the critical area of the distribution. It sets the "cutoff" for what counts as significant.

Q2: If statistical power is 0.93, what does this mean?

If the null hypothesis is false, there is a 93% chance of rejecting the null hypothesis.

Q3: What is the minimum power threshold considered acceptable?

80% (or 0.80). If power is less than 80%, there is insufficient power.

Q4: What is the practical use of statistical power?

Power analysis tells researchers how many participants are needed to conduct a study with findings they can trust.

Q5: When alpha increases, what else increases?

Both statistical power AND probability of Type I error increase when alpha increases.

Complete Quiz Walkthrough: Chandler Household Study

The Data

Population (City of Chandler): $\mu = 3.1$, $\sigma = 1.6$

Sample (Your Neighborhood): 16 households with sizes: 2, 4, 2, 6, 3, 2, 4, 4, 3, 5, 6, 2, 3, 5, 3, 2

Research Question: Is the neighborhood different from the city? (non-directional)

Alpha: $\alpha = 0.10$ (two-tailed)

Step 1: Identify DV and IV

DV (what we measure): Household size

IV (what separates groups): Location (Chandler vs. Neighborhood)

Step 2: State the Null Hypothesis

Since this is non-directional ("different", not "higher/lower"):

"Household sizes in my neighborhood are not significantly different from the general household sizes in Chandler."

Step 3: Calculate Sample Mean ($M$)

Sum = 2+4+2+6+3+2+4+4+3+5+6+2+3+5+3+2 = 56

$M = 56 / 16 = \mathbf{3.5}$

Step 4: Calculate Standard Error ($SE$)

$SE = \sigma / \sqrt{N} = 1.6 / \sqrt{16} = 1.6 / 4 = \mathbf{0.4}$

Step 5: Calculate Z-Statistic

$Z = (M - \mu) / SE = (3.5 - 3.1) / 0.4 = 0.4 / 0.4 = \mathbf{1.0}$

Step 6: Determine Tails & Critical Value

Non-directional = Two-tailed

At $\alpha = 0.10$, Two-tailed: Critical Z = ¬±1.645

Step 7: Make the Decision

Is |1.0| > 1.645? No. 1.0 is less extreme than 1.645.

Decision: Fail to Reject the null hypothesis.

Conclusion: "The household sizes in my neighborhood are NOT significantly different from the city of Chandler."

Step 8: Calculate Effect Size (Cohen's d)

$d = (M - \mu) / \sigma = (3.5 - 3.1) / 1.6 = 0.4 / 1.6 = \mathbf{0.25}$

This is a small effect (below 0.5).

Practice A: One-Tailed (Directional) Test

Scenario: Coffee Shop Wait Times

A coffee chain reports that the national average wait time is Œº = 4.5 minutes with œÉ = 1.2 minutes. A manager believes her store has faster service. She records wait times for 25 customers and finds M = 4.1 minutes. Test at Œ± = .05.

1. Identify the Test Type

Keywords: "faster" = directional prediction ‚Üí One-Tailed Test

Direction: Predicting lower wait times

2. Write Hypotheses

$H_0: \mu_{store} \ge 4.5$ (Store is same or slower)

$H_1: \mu_{store} < 4.5$ (Store is faster)

Note: $H_1$ matches the prediction direction

3. Calculate SE

$SE = \sigma / \sqrt{N} = 1.2 / \sqrt{25} = 1.2 / 5 = \mathbf{0.24}$

4. Calculate Z

$Z = (M - \mu) / SE = (4.1 - 4.5) / 0.24 = -0.4 / 0.24 = \mathbf{-1.67}$

5. Find Critical Z

One-tailed, Œ± = .05, predicting lower ‚Üí Critical Z = -1.645

(Negative because we predict the sample is BELOW the population)

6. Decision

Is -1.67 more extreme than -1.645? Yes! (-1.67 is further from zero)

Reject the null. The store's wait times are significantly faster.

7. Effect Size

$d = (M - \mu) / \sigma = (4.1 - 4.5) / 1.2 = -0.4 / 1.2 = \mathbf{-0.33}$ (Small-Medium effect)

Practice B: Two-Tailed (Non-Directional) Test

Scenario: Smartphone Battery Life

A manufacturer claims phones last Œº = 18 hours on average with œÉ = 3.5 hours. A reviewer tests 50 phones and finds M = 17.2 hours. Is this significantly different? Test at Œ± = .05.

1. Identify the Test Type

Keywords: "significantly different" = no direction ‚Üí Two-Tailed Test

2. Write Hypotheses

$H_0: \mu_{tested} = 18$ (No difference)

$H_1: \mu_{tested} \neq 18$ (There is a difference)

3. Calculate SE

$SE = 3.5 / \sqrt{50} = 3.5 / 7.07 = \mathbf{0.495}$

4. Calculate Z

$Z = (17.2 - 18) / 0.495 = -0.8 / 0.495 = \mathbf{-1.62}$

5. Find Critical Z

Two-tailed, Œ± = .05 ‚Üí Critical Z = ¬±1.96

6. Decision

Is |-1.62| > 1.96? No. 1.62 < 1.96

Fail to reject. Battery life is not significantly different from the claim.

Practice C: Sample Size Effects

Key Concept: How Sample Size Affects Results

When you run the same test with a larger sample size:

What Changes?
  • SE gets smaller (because you divide œÉ by a larger ‚àöN)
  • Z gets larger (because you divide by a smaller SE)
  • More likely to reject (Z more likely to exceed critical value)
What Stays the Same?
  • Effect Size (Cohen's d) - because the formula $d = (M - \mu) / \sigma$ has NO N in it!
  • The effect size measures the actual magnitude of difference, regardless of sample size
Example Comparison
Measure N = 20 N = 100
SE (if œÉ = 10) 10/‚àö20 = 2.24 10/‚àö100 = 1.00
Z (if M-Œº = 3) 3/2.24 = 1.34 3/1.00 = 3.00
Cohen's d 3/10 = 0.30 3/10 = 0.30
Decision (Œ±=.05, 2-tail) Fail to Reject Reject

Takeaway: Larger samples increase power (ability to detect effects), but don't change the actual effect size.
