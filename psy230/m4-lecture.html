Why do we need a "t" test?

In Module 3, every problem provided the Population Standard Deviation ($\sigma$). But in the real world, if you are studying a new drug or a specific sub-group, you don't know the population's spread. You only have your sample.

The Problem of Estimation

We can use our Sample Standard Deviation ($s$) to estimate the Population Standard Deviation ($\sigma$). However, samples tend to be less variable than the full population (we rarely catch the extreme outliers in a small sample).

If we just used $s$ in a Z-test formula, we would underestimate the error and get false positives (Type I errors).

The Solution: The t-Distribution

The t-distribution is a "shape-shifter." It looks like a normal Z-curve, but it changes shape based on sample size.

Z-Distribution vs. t-Distribution

  • Z-Distribution: Perfect Normal Bell Curve. Used when $\sigma$ is known.
  • t-Distribution: Has Fatter Tails. This accounts for the uncertainty of estimating $\sigma$.
  • As Sample Size ($N$) gets bigger, the t-distribution looks more and more like the Z-distribution.

Why fatter tails matter: More area in the tails means the critical cutoff values are more extreme. With the same Î± = .05, the cutoff for t is farther from zero than Z (e.g., Â±2.306 for df=8 vs. Â±1.96 for Z). This makes t-tests harder to reach significance â€” which is appropriate because we have more uncertainty.

What is a "Conservative" Test?
When scientists call a hypothesis test conservative, they mean it is difficult to reject the null hypothesis. The t-test is more conservative than the Z-test because:
  • The fatter tails push critical values further from zero
  • Your calculated t must be more extreme to reach significance
  • This protects against false positives when we have uncertainty in our estimates

Assumptions of t-Tests

T-tests are parametric tests, meaning they have specific requirements:

The Three Assumptions

  1. The DV is a scale variable â€” The dependent variable must be measured at the interval or ratio level (not nominal or ordinal). You can't run a t-test on categories!
  2. The population is normally distributed â€” The data should come from a normally distributed population. However, t-tests are "robust" to violations with large samples (N > 30).
  3. Participants are randomly selected â€” Ideally, participants should be randomly selected from the population. Note: This assumption is often violated in practice (most studies use convenience samples), but it affects generalizability, not the math.

Knowledge Check: Z vs. t-Test

Question: A researcher wants to compare the average IQ of psychology majors ($\mu = 100$ is known) to the national average. The researcher collects a sample of 25 students and calculates the sample standard deviation from the data. Which test should be used?

One-Sample t-Test

Even though the population mean is known ($\mu = 100$), the population standard deviation is NOT known. The researcher must calculate it from the sample, so we use a t-test to account for the uncertainty in estimating $\sigma$.

The One-Sample Calculation Engine

The formula for $t$ looks almost identical to $Z$, but the symbols change to reflect that we are estimating.

1. Degrees of Freedom ($df$)

The shape of the t-distribution is determined by Degrees of Freedom. For a one-sample test:

$df = N - 1$

Example: If you test 10 cars, $df = 9$. You look up the critical value in the row for 9.

2. Calculate Sample Statistics

A. Estimated Population Standard Deviation ($s$)
Note the denominator is $N-1$ (Degrees of Freedom), not $N$. This makes the SD slightly larger to correct for bias.

$s = \sqrt{\frac{\sum(X - M)^2}{N - 1}}$

Excel Formula: =STDEV.S(data) (The "S" stands for Sample, using N-1)

B. Estimated Standard Error ($SE$ or $s_M$)
We use the estimated SD ($s$) to find the standard error.

$SE = \frac{s}{\sqrt{N}}$

Excel Formula: =s/SQRT(N)

C. The t-Statistic

$t = \frac{M - \mu}{SE}$

3. Anatomy of the t-Statistic Formula

Understanding what's in the numerator vs. denominator is a common quiz question!

Breaking Down the Formula

Part Formula Component What It Represents
Numerator
(Top)		 $M - \mu$ A difference between means: How far is the sample mean from the population mean?
Denominator
(Bottom)		 $SE = s / \sqrt{N}$ The estimated standard error of the sampling distribution of the mean

In plain English: The t-statistic tells you how many standard errors the sample mean is from the population mean.

Quiz Pattern: "The numerator of all t statistics contains ____"
Answer: A difference between means

"The denominator of the one-sample t statistic is ____"
Answer: The estimated standard error of the sampling distribution of the mean

Paired-Samples t-Test

Also called "Dependent-Means," "Repeated-Measures," or "Within-Subjects" t-test.

When to use it?

Use this when data points are linked:

  • Pre/Post: Measuring anxiety before and after a yoga class for the same people.
  • Matched Pairs: Comparing twins (Twin A vs Twin B).
  • Repeated Conditions: Each person tastes Cola A and Cola B.
  • Longitudinal: Measuring the same people at age 70 and again at age 80.
Why Use Paired-Samples Instead of Two Separate Groups?
When the assignment asks "Explain why a paired-samples t test is appropriate," your answer should include:
  • The same participants are measured twice (OR participants are matched one-to-one)
  • Each data point in one condition has a corresponding linked data point in the other condition
  • The scores are not independent â€” they come from the same person or matched pair

Example answer: "A paired-samples t test is appropriate because the same patients were measured both before and after the treatment. Each person's post-treatment score is directly linked to their own pre-treatment score, making the data points dependent rather than independent."

The "Difference Score" Trick

The math for a Paired-Samples t-test is actually just a One-Sample t-test in disguise!

  1. We don't analyze Score 1 and Score 2 separately.
  2. We subtract them to create a Difference Score ($D$).
  3. We run a One-Sample t-test on the Difference Scores, comparing them to a population mean of Zero.

The Comparison Distribution

What is the Comparison Distribution for Paired-Samples?

In a paired-samples t test, the comparison distribution is a distribution of mean difference scores.

  • We're not comparing individual scores or group means
  • We're comparing our sample's mean difference to a distribution of all possible mean differences
  • The center of this distribution is $\mu_{diff} = 0$ (if the null hypothesis is true)

Quiz Answer: "The comparison distribution in a paired-samples t test is a distribution of mean difference scores."

The Null Hypothesis Logic

The Logic

Null Hypothesis ($H_0$): The average difference is Zero ($\mu_{diff} = 0$).
(Meaning: The yoga class changed nothing).

Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$): The average difference is NOT Zero ($\mu_{diff} \neq 0$).

What to Expect if the Null Hypothesis is True?
This is a common quiz question! If the null hypothesis is true (no real effect), you would expect:
  • The mean difference score to be around zero
  • Some people might show positive differences, some negative, but they cancel out
  • Any deviation from zero is just random chance

Example (Weight Loss Program): If the null is true for a paired-samples t test about weight loss, you would expect "no change in weight or weight gain, indicating that the program does not work."

Key Insight: A paired-samples t-test is essentially asking: "Is the average difference significantly different from zero?" If the differences are consistently positive (or negative), that suggests a real effect.

Step 2: Find the Mean of Differences ($M_{diff}$)

Add up the difference column and divide by N.

$M_{diff} = \frac{\sum D}{N}$
Step 3: Find SD of Differences ($s_{diff}$)

Calculate the standard deviation of that difference column (using N-1).

$s_{diff} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum(D - M_{diff})^2}{N - 1}}$

$SE_{diff} = \frac{s_{diff}}{\sqrt{N}}$
Step 5: Calculate t
$t = \frac{M_{diff} - 0}{SE_{diff}} = \frac{M_{diff}}{SE_{diff}}$

Note: We subtract 0 because the Null Hypothesis assumes zero difference.

Common Mistake: Do NOT calculate the mean of Group A and the mean of Group B and try to run a test on those two numbers. You MUST calculate the differences row-by-row first!

Decisions & Effect Size

1. Finding the Critical Value

You need three things to find the cutoff in a t-table:

  1. Alpha ($\alpha$): Usually .05 or .10.
  2. Tails: One-tailed (directional) or Two-tailed (non-directional)?
  3. Degrees of Freedom ($df$): $N - 1$.

Example: $N=10$, $\alpha=.05$, Two-tailed. Look up $df=9$. Critical $t = \pm 2.262$.

Using the t-Table: Unlike the Z-table which has fixed values, the t-table changes based on degrees of freedom. Smaller $df$ means larger critical values (harder to reject). As $df$ increases, critical t-values approach Z-values.

If your calculated $t$ is more extreme (larger magnitude) than the critical value, Reject Null.

Decision Example

3. Reading p-Values in Statistical Notation

In research articles and some quiz questions, you'll see results reported in this format:

$t(df) = t\text{-statistic}, p = \text{exact p-value}$

How to Read This Notation

Example: $t(12) = 2.2, p = .03$

  • $t(12)$ = t-test with 12 degrees of freedom (meaning N = 13)
  • $= 2.2$ = the calculated t-statistic is 2.2
  • $p = .03$ = the probability of getting this result by chance is 3%
The p-Value Decision Rule
  • If $p < \alpha$ â†’ Reject the null hypothesis (result is significant)
  • If $p \geq \alpha$ â†’ Fail to reject the null hypothesis (result is not significant)

Using $\alpha = .05$:

  • $t(12) = 2.2, p = .03$ â†’ Reject null (because .03 < .05)
  • $t(11) = 2.2, p = .11$ â†’ Fail to reject (because .11 > .05)
  • $t(4) = 2.2, p = .09$ â†’ Fail to reject (because .09 > .05)

4. Effect Size (Cohen's d)

Just like in Module 3, we calculate how big the effect is. But be careful with the formula!

$d = \frac{M - \mu}{s}$
CRITICAL DISTINCTION:
For the t-statistic, the denominator is Standard Error ($SE$).
For Cohen's d, the denominator is Standard Deviation ($s$).
Effect size is about the distribution of scores, not the distribution of means!

Effect Size for Paired-Samples

For paired-samples tests, use the standard deviation of the differences:

$d = \frac{M_{diff}}{s_{diff}}$

Example (Yoga): $d = -1.75 / 1.16 = \mathbf{-1.51}$ (Large effect)

Knowledge Check: Effect Size Calculation

Question: Imagine the average time to complete a 4-year bachelor degree is actually 4.7 years, based on national data. You collect data on the 68 psychology students who started school during the same semester as you, finding an average time to complete at 4.3 years with a standard deviation of 0.6 years. What would be the effect size associated with a single-sample t test?

$d = (M - \mu) / s = (4.3 - 4.7) / 0.6 = -0.4 / 0.6 = \mathbf{-0.67}$

This is a medium effect size. The negative sign indicates that psychology students complete faster than the national average.

Remember: Effect size uses $s$ (standard deviation), NOT $SE$ (standard error)!

5. Confidence Intervals

A Confidence Interval (CI) gives us a range of likely values for the population mean.

The Zero Rule

If you are doing a Paired-Samples test (or One-Sample comparing to zero):

  • If the 95% CI includes 0 (e.g., [-2.5, 4.3]): Fail to Reject. No significant effect.
  • If the 95% CI does NOT include 0 (e.g., [1.2, 5.5]): Reject Null. Significant effect.
$CI = M_{diff} \pm (t_{critical} \times SE_{diff})$

Confidence Interval Example

Using the yoga example: $M_{diff} = -1.75$, $SE_{diff} = 0.41$, $t_{critical} = 2.365$ (df=7, Î±=.05, two-tailed)

$CI = -1.75 \pm (2.365 \times 0.41)$

$CI = -1.75 \pm 0.97$

$CI = [-2.72, -0.78]$

Interpretation: We are 95% confident that the true mean difference in anxiety scores is between -2.72 and -0.78. Since this interval does NOT include 0, we reject the null hypothesis.

Knowledge Check: Confidence Intervals

Question: A researcher calculates a 95% confidence interval for a paired-samples t-test and finds [-0.5, 2.3]. What should the researcher conclude?

Fail to Reject the Null Hypothesis

The confidence interval includes 0 (the range goes from -0.5 to 2.3, which contains 0). This means we cannot be confident that there is a real difference. The null hypothesis (that the mean difference equals zero) cannot be rejected.

Practice Scenario: Laptop Battery Life

Situation: The industry average battery life for laptops is 10 hours ($\mu=10$). You want to know if "Brand X" is significantly different. You test a sample of 10 Brand X laptops.

Sample Data: 11, 12, 10, 9, 8, 13, 11, 10, 11, 14.

Hypotheses (Two-Tailed):
$H_0: \mu_{BrandX} = 10$
$H_1: \mu_{BrandX} \neq 10$

Steps:

  1. Calculate Sample Mean ($M$).
  2. Calculate Estimated Pop SD ($s$). (Use Excel `STDEV.S` or manual $N-1$).
  3. Calculate SE ($s / \sqrt{10}$).
  4. Calculate $t$ ($ (M - 10) / SE $).
  5. Find Critical $t$ ($df=9, \alpha=.10$).
  6. Decide (Is calc $t$ > critical $t$?).
  7. Effect Size ($ (M - 10) / s $).
Step-by-Step Solution

Step 1: Calculate M
Sum = 11+12+10+9+8+13+11+10+11+14 = 109
$M = 109 / 10 = \mathbf{10.9}$

Step 2: Calculate s
Using Excel STDEV.S or formula: $s = \mathbf{1.79}$

Step 3: Calculate SE
$SE = 1.79 / \sqrt{10} = 1.79 / 3.16 = \mathbf{0.566}$

Step 4: Calculate t
$t = (10.9 - 10) / 0.566 = 0.9 / 0.566 = \mathbf{1.59}$

Step 5: Find Critical t
$df = 10 - 1 = 9$, $\alpha = .10$, Two-tailed
Critical $t = \pm \mathbf{1.833}$

Step 6: Decision
Is |1.59| > 1.833? No. Fail to Reject.

Step 7: Effect Size
$d = (10.9 - 10) / 1.79 = 0.9 / 1.79 = \mathbf{0.50}$ (Medium effect)

$H_1: \mu_{diff} \neq 0$ (There is a difference)

Step-by-Step Solution

Step 1: Calculate Differences (A - B)
Differences: 3, 3, 4, -2, 4, 2, 2, 5

Step 2: Calculate M_diff
$M_{diff} = (3 + 3 + 4 + -2 + 4 + 2 + 2 + 5) / 8 = 21 / 8 = \mathbf{2.625}$

Step 3: Calculate s_diff
$s_{diff} = \mathbf{2.20}$ (using STDEV.S)

Step 4: Calculate SE
$SE_{diff} = 2.20 / \sqrt{8} = 2.20 / 2.83 = \mathbf{0.78}$

Step 5: Calculate t
$t = 2.625 / 0.78 = \mathbf{3.37}$

Step 6: Find Critical t
$df = 7$, $\alpha = .05$, Two-tailed
Critical $t = \mathbf{\pm 2.365}$

Step 7: Decision
Is |3.37| > 2.365? Yes!
Reject Null. There is a significant difference in reading comprehension between the two conditions.

Step 8: Effect Size
$d = 2.625 / 2.20 = \mathbf{1.19}$ (Large effect)

Interpretation: Students scored significantly higher in silence than with background music.
